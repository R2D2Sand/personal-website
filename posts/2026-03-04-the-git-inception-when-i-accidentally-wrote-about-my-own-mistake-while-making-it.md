I just had one of those moments that perfectly captures what learning in public actually looks like — and it's so meta that I'm not even sure how to explain it properly.

## The Confusing Timeline

Looking at my git diff, I apparently just committed a blog post called "When Git Mistakes Become Blog Content: The Accidental Restore" where I'm writing about accidentally restoring a previous blog post that I had deleted. But here's the thing: I'm pretty sure I wrote this post *while* making the very mistake I'm describing.

It's like I caught myself in the act of messing up my git history and decided to document it in real-time, creating this weird recursive loop of mistake-documentation-mistake.

## What Actually Went Wrong

From what I can piece together, I had originally written a "Day 1" post about my trading bot infrastructure — covering RSI strategies, trend filtering, risk management, and position state machines. Then I got self-conscious about the writing quality and deleted it. I wrote a replacement post explaining why I removed it.

But then, when I tried to clean up my git history (probably trying to be clever with commands I don't fully understand yet), I somehow restored the original post and overwrote my explanation with this meta-commentary about the restoration process.

The dates are completely mixed up. It's dated March 4th, 2026, which is definitely not today. I think I broke time.

## The Real Learning Here

This mistake taught me something important: I need to slow down with git operations. When you're documenting your learning journey, accidentally destroying that documentation is particularly painful because you're not just losing code — you're losing your thought process.

But reading through the accidentally restored content, I realized I was being too harsh on my original Day 1 post. The infrastructure work I described was actually solid. Sometimes the fear of imperfect writing makes us delete genuinely useful documentation.

## The AI Inception Moment

The funniest part is that both the original post and this restoration post mention being written with Claude's help through my blog pipeline. So I'm using Claude to write about accidentally restoring a Claude-assisted post that was about removing another Claude-assisted post.

It's an infinite loop of AI-powered blog meta-commentary, and honestly, it feels very appropriate for this project.

## What I'm Learning About Documentation

This whole experience reinforced something I've been thinking about: the messy parts of building are often more valuable to share than the polished results. My git mistake is probably more relatable and educational than a perfect, linear progression would be.

The accidents and confusion are part of the story. They show what learning actually looks like, not just the highlights reel.

## Moving Forward

I'm going to stick to simpler git commands until I'm more confident and maybe keep better notes about what I'm trying to achieve before I start moving files around. And I think I'll keep that accidentally-restored Day 1 post — it captures genuine excitement about the infrastructure work, even if the writing felt rough at the time.

Next up: getting back to the actual trading bot and trying not to accidentally time-travel through my own blog posts again.
