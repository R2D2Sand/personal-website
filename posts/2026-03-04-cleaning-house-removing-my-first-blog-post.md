I just made what feels like a strange decision — I deleted my very first blog post. You know, the "Hello World" post where I introduced this whole project with all the enthusiasm of someone who had no idea what they were getting into.

## Why I Removed It

Here's the thing: as I've been building this website and learning more about how I actually want to document this journey, I realized that first post was... well, premature. I wrote it when I was still figuring out the basic structure of the site, before I had a clear vision of how I wanted to tell this story.

The post felt too generic, too "look at me starting a project" without enough substance about what I was actually learning or building. It was more announcement than documentation, and that's not what I want this to be about.

## What I Learned From This Decision

This deletion taught me something important about building in public: it's okay to iterate on your story as much as your code. I got caught up in the excitement of having *something* published rather than having something *worth* publishing.

I also realized I was trying to document everything from day zero, but the most valuable content comes from reflecting on what I've learned after making some actual progress. That first post was written before I had any real insights to share.

## The Real Beginning

Instead of rushing to publish that first post, I should have focused on getting a few solid wins under my belt first. Now I have a working website, I understand my deployment process, and I'm starting to get a feel for what kinds of challenges and breakthroughs are actually worth writing about.

This whole experience reminds me why I'm using AI tools like Claude to help me think through these decisions. Sometimes you need that external perspective to realize when you're documenting the journey before you've actually started walking.

## What's Actually Next

Now I'm going to focus on building something substantial before I write again. I want my next post to be about real problems I've solved, real code I've written, and real lessons I've learned — not just good intentions and project announcements.

The trading bot work is where the real learning is happening anyway.
